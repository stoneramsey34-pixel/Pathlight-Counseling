What EMDR is — and why it works

Talk therapy doesn't always reach where trauma lives. EMDR does.

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is one of the most thoroughly researched and widely endorsed treatments for trauma in the world. It's recommended by the American Psychological Association, the World Health Organization, and the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs for treatment of PTSD and trauma.

Unlike traditional talk therapy, EMDR doesn't require you to repeatedly tell the story of what happened. Instead, it uses bilateral stimulation — typically guided eye movements — to help your brain reprocess traumatic memories in a way that reduces their emotional charge. What was stuck becomes unstuck.

At Pathlight Counseling in Woodstock, GA, Dr. Michael Reece Carpenter leads our EMDR practice. He works with clients whose trauma — whether from a single event or years of difficult experiences — is still affecting how they live today.

Who EMDR can help

EMDR is effective for a wide range of experiences

→PTSD from a specific traumatic event (accident, assault, combat, loss)
→Childhood trauma or adverse experiences that still affect you as an adult
→Addiction with roots in unprocessed trauma
→Anxiety, phobias, or panic with a clear experiential root
→Grief and loss that hasn't resolved through traditional therapy
→Intrusive memories, flashbacks, or nightmares that won't stop
